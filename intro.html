Highland Cattle

They are the oldest registry feed cattle on the planet!

Highland, an ancient Scottish breed, is the oldest registered cattle breed.

Doubled-Layered Coat

They have about 13 inches long hair with a slight wave. Their double-layered coat can prevent rain seeping into their skin with the outer oiled hair and warm their body with the downy undercoat.

Horns

They use their long and strong horns to dig into pastures covered with heavy snow and forage for food during winters.

Health

With great adaptability for the environments and protection from their generic features, they do have great longevity, which can be as long as 20 years with 12 calves on average.

Colour

Their best-known colour of coat is ginger-red, as well as black, brindle, yellow and white.

Calving

Highland cattle are usually able to mate at about 18 months of age, and pregnancy in Highland cows usually lasts up to 290 days.

Protective Nature

These cattle are quite docile, but can be protective if their young are threatened. For this reason, it’s always important to take extra care around all cows and particularly bulls, regardless of breed!

Meat and Commercial Values

Since they retain their body heat by having a thick coat and not by storing excess fat, their meat is quite lean. When cross-bred with larger sires such as Shorthorn or Limousin, their meat becomes much more commercially desirable.

The Earliest Breeds Registration

In Scotland, the Highland cattle registry was established in 1885. Archeological evidence shows that the breed existed in the sixth century. Some written records show that Highland cattle existed in the twelfth century.

Exportation

It’s known for its extraordinary adaptations for harsh environmental conditions including severe climate, large amount of rainfall, strong winds, and snowy winter. Highland cattle have been exported to many countries such as Canada, Northway, north America, south America, Finland, Australia, New Zealand etc.

The American Highland Cattle Association

The American Scotch Highland Breeders Association was formed in August 1948. Later, in 1992, the name was changed to the American Highland Cattle Association or AHCA. The American Highland Cattle Association registry was first formed in 1948.
